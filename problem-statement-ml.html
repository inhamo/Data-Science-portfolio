Data Science - Problem Statement Formulating

Is there a clear, actionable business problem or objective?

This is the first step where you identify if there is a specific problem or goal that can guide your project.

Example: "Increase website traffic."

Define it in measurable terms

Make sure the objective is quantifiable. This will help in setting KPIs and measuring success later on. Be clear with your definitons as meanings may vary per company and per industry i.e. vulnerable client in banking is different from a vulnerable client in health

Example: "Achieve a monthly sales revenue of $100,000."

Collaborate with stakeholders for clarification

Engage with relevant stakeholders to gather insights and refine the problem statement. Collaboration is key to understanding the needs of different parties.

Example: "Meet with the marketing team to discuss current challenges and opportunities."

Which metric are we optimizing?

Identify the specific metrics that align with your business goals. Metrics like revenue, churn rate, and customer lifetime value (CLV) are common targets.

Example: "We aim to optimize our customer acquisition cost to be below $50 per customer."

Ask: What are the business's main pain points?

Identify the primary challenges or obstacles that the business faces. Understanding pain points is essential for developing effective solutions.

Example: "High customer churn rate is leading to reduced revenue."

Do we have existing business hypotheses?

Assess if there are pre-existing hypotheses about the business problem that can guide your analysis. This can provide a starting point for your work.

Example: "We believe that improving customer service will reduce churn by 20%."

List the assumptions and expected outcomes

Document any assumptions and the anticipated results of your hypotheses. This will help in assessing the validity of your approach.

Example: "Assuming better training for staff will lead to improved customer satisfaction and lower churn."

Plan for Exploratory Data Analysis to form hypotheses

If there are no existing hypotheses, outline a plan for exploratory data analysis (EDA) to identify potential insights and hypotheses.

Example: "Analyze customer feedback and service interaction data to uncover trends."

Can these hypotheses be tested with the available data?

Evaluate if the current data can support testing your hypotheses. Validating hypotheses requires adequate data.

Example: "We have sufficient data from customer feedback surveys to test our hypothesis."

Define testable questions to move forward

Formulate specific questions that can be tested with your data. Clear questions guide your analysis effectively.

Example: "What is the relationship between customer service ratings and churn rate?"

Reassess the data or business question

If the data is insufficient, reconsider your data sources or the business problem itself. Adjustments may be necessary for clarity.

Example: "The data does not cover all customer interactions; we need to gather more information."

Are the necessary data sources identified and accessible?

Ensure that you have access to all the required data sources to conduct your analysis. Accessibility is crucial for smooth progress.

Example: "We have access to customer feedback data, sales data, and service logs."

Is customer data being used and does it involve personal identifiable information?

Evaluate if the customer data you are using contains any personal identifiable information (PII) and ensure compliance with regulations.

Example: "Are we storing names, addresses, or contact information?"

Proceed with non-sensitive data

If data access is an issue, consider working with non-sensitive data as a temporary solution to continue your analysis.

Example: "Utilize anonymized customer feedback data for initial analysis."

Is the data anonymized or can it be anonymized?

Check if the data can be anonymized or if it is already anonymized to ensure the privacy of individuals.

Example: "Can we mask or aggregate the data?"

Proceed with anonymized data

If the data can be anonymized, you can move forward safely with your analysis.

Example: "Anonymized data is ready for use in analysis."

Proceed without PII

If the data does not include PII, you can proceed with your analysis without additional concerns.

Example: "Data is aggregated and does not contain personal details."

Apply anonymization techniques

If the data cannot be anonymized, consider applying techniques like masking or generalization to protect individual identities.

Example: "Masking personal identifiers before analysis."

Is the data sufficient in terms of quantity, quality, and relevance?

Ensure that your data is adequate for analysis. Insufficient data can lead to unreliable conclusions.

Example: "Do we have enough records and diverse data points?"

Prepare for data collection and preprocessing

If the data is sufficient, proceed with preparing the data for analysis. Ensure all necessary cleaning and formatting steps are taken.

Example: "Data is collected and ready for preprocessing."

Identify additional data sources or improve data quality

If the data is insufficient, consider finding additional sources or enhancing the quality of existing data.

Example: "Look for alternative data sources or improve data collection methods."
